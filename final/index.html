Sports Gambling: The Risk is Outweighing the Reward

When legislation passed in November 2021, Maryland joined multiple other states to legalize and launch sports betting.

The new set of laws opened up opportunities for Maryland residents to place wagers in-person at casinos and racetracks, and online through popular sports betting companies like Caesars Sportsbook and FanDuel.

The state has handled over $2 billion worth of wagers since the passing of legislation, according to PlayMaryland. Anyone over the age of 21 can place bets in Maryland, while other states may allow anyone over 18-years-old to gamble.


Youth and Gambling

Young adults, like college students, are found to risk their money the most. Around 75% of college students participate in betting, according to studies done on the prevalence of sports betting on college campuses.

Online betting platforms make it easier for anyone to participate, contributing to the upward trend of young adults placing bets on their favorite sports teams and leagues. Research shows bets being placed in lotteries and card games alongside games of skill, like basketball or golf.

A budding problem, the National Collegiate Athletic Association conducted their own study to examine sports betting behavior among college-aged adults (18-22-years-old). NCAA President Charlie Baker authorized the survey to better understand what is driving young adults to gamble, and provide resources to help those who are developing unhealthy habits.

The NCAA reviewed data from 3,527 young adults, including student-athletes and normal students, to “better understand the prevalence of various sports wagering behaviors.” Of those young adults, over half said that they performed in some type of sports betting action.

About 15% of those surveyed place at least one bet per month and 4% participate in wagering activity every day, according to the survey conducted in April 2023.

Risky Behavior

When young adults are participating in gambling activities, they are more likely to engage in risky behaviors when gambling.

The NCAA outlined three actions that are defined as risky behaviors: betting either a few times a week or daily, betting $50 or more in a typical wager and losing more than $500 betting on sports in a single day.

Sixteen percent said they find themselves participating in one of those three activities, largely due to the ease of placing a wager. More than half of the respondents to the NCAA survey use a form of online sports betting service to make bets.

Online betting platforms are relatively new to the sports betting industry. Intertops opened its doors on the internet for U.S. customers in 1996. FanDuel then began operations online in 2009, and many others followed suit. There are now 40 legal sportsbooks in operation.

Effects of Advertisements

Advertisements aren't a new concept. But, those encouraging sports gambling are.

An estimated $300 million was spent on sports gambling publicity last year. Sportsbook advertisements can be seen on television during commercial breaks, but social media ads are becoming more prevalent as well.

In the same NCAA study, over half of the young adults questioned recalled seeing or hearing a sports gambling related advertisement. Those online messages pushed the idea of wagering by ways of an online sportsbook.

And it's working. 52% of young adults are more likely to place a bet on a sporting event after observing an advertisement.

Sports betting has taken over the sports landscape, and college-aged students are the prime demographic of those participating. More states are on the verge of passing legislation to legalize gambling, quickly paving the way for more to become involved.

The sports betting scene is rapidly changing. More young adults are beginning to participate as the risks soar.
